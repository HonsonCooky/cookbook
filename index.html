Growing up in a family obsessed with music and theatre, I spent years on stage before calculus and physics completely hijacked my life plan. Somewhere between the performances and the problem sets, I fell head-first into computers - the result is a wild mix of showman and nerd.

These days I'm leading backend development at Splitting Point Studios, building Go services that help bring ambitious multiplayer game ideas to life. Before that, I was a software engineer at a large SaaS platform and a major fuel distributor, both based in New Zealand.

All of that followed a Bachelor of Engineering with First Class Honours, majoring in Software Engineering with a specialisation in Networked Applications - quite the mouthful, I know, haha.

For me there isn't much of a line between the work and the rest of my life. The game engines I build for fun, the PCs I put together, and the low-level systems I explore in Rust all circle the same question I chase at work: what should software be able to do, and what shouldn't it? If you want the longer version of how I think about it, it's in Wired to Ask.

Interests

Most of what I do in my own time isn't really separate from my work - it's the same curiosity, pointed in different directions. I've written about how I tend to think in Wired to Ask; what follows is just where that goes when the problem is one I picked myself.

Game Engines

Building my own game engine isn't about reinventing the wheel so much as reinventing the workflow. Mainstream engines are tightly coupled to their own way of working - what lets them scale, but also what asks for more surface knowledge than I've ever been good at carrying. Building it from the ground up, with AI in the loop, is how I go looking for a smaller surface area: one shaped to how I actually think.

Hardware

I've built my own PC, but what stays with me isn't the spec sheet - it's the intuition for what modern hardware can actually do. By that measure something doesn't add up: today's machines are well beyond what last decade's games needed, yet current titles still bring strong hardware to its knees. I read it as an optimisation problem, just not the usual one - the industry pours effort into the low level while the high-level wins go untouched. A lot of careful work, aimed at the wrong layer.

Ergo Keyboards

This one began as a fix rather than a hobby. A childhood elbow injury left my right wrist with limited rotation - a non-issue at school, until full-time keyboard work turned it into a real one. Solving it pulled me deep into ergonomic boards, through a Planck EZ and a Glove80 and a fair amount of trial and error, and landed me on a ZSA Voyager, which has been my keyboard ever since. Being able to rest my elbow properly again changed things, and I've grown genuinely passionate about the space.

Linux

I enjoy driving Linux as my daily OS, and when a workflow suits it, that's where I work. I hold it loosely, though - I move between Linux and Windows depending on what the task in front of me actually wants. The control Linux gives is the part I genuinely enjoy; the willingness to switch is just pragmatism.

The Outdoors

Getting outdoors, hiking most often, is where my best ideas tend to surface. Stepping away from the screen gives them room to breathe and connect in ways they never quite do while I'm staring straight at the problem. It reads as time off, but it's closer to a slower way of doing the same work.
